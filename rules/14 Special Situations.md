# Special Situations

## Surprise, Confrontations, and Initiative

### Surprise

Surprise occurs when a target is unaware of incoming danger, such as an ambush, stealth attack or enemies bursting from hiding. Surprised characters have their choice of actions restricted.

Characters Surprised by danger gain the "Combat Panic(4)" SpecialFx and must test against it.

The initiative still works at normal for the purposes of determining turn order.

### Confrontation

A confrontation occurs when both sides are aware or spot each other at the same time. All participants make an initiative (INI) Test.

- Initiative Test use a number of dice equal to the INI Attribute.
- They roll using the core rules. Pick the highest dice and extra sixes add +1.
- Ties act simultaneously on the first round, attacks exchanged simultaneously take effect after the both acts are resolved.

The next turn the initiative works as normal for the purposes of determining turn order.

## Running, Chases and Pursuits

Chases are a staple of the action genre. The following procedure provides a guideline for running Chases using an Effort Test:

### Setting up the Chase

Define The distance:

- The total Effort to reach the goal equals the distance in hexes (meters for characters).

Establish Starting Positions:

- The last character starts at effort 0.
- Characters that start the chase ahead get free effort from starting ahead, usually 1 Effort per hex ahead.

Determine Chase Modifiers:

- Chase modifier equals Sprint-6 meters.
- The Running Skill adds its CPX to the Chase modifier.

Note: Normal character sprint is 6+FIT, making the chase modifier equal to the Fitness Modifier, unless a SpecialFx apply.

### Running the Chase

- Make a Fitness Attribute Test or use the Running Skill.
- Add the Chase modifier to the roll.
- Fumbles simply mean the character advances 0 Effort.

### Ending the Chase

- Characters who accumulate the required Effort reach their goal first.

### Pursuing Someone

If the chase involves pursuers and targets, define who is being pursued.

- If the pursued character reaches the required Effort first, they escape.
- If the pursuers surpass the current Effort of their target at any point, they catch up.

Pursuers can then make Actions to stop their target. Ranged Attacks are considered to be done at Short Distance, using normal modifiers.

- If the target's movement is stopped or reduced by injury, the chase is over.

### Being Too Slow/Fast

If two characters differ by more than 6 in their Chase modifiers. Their speeds are too different for a chase to occur. In these cases the Referee can simply rule that the faster character automatically succeeds.

### Obstacles in the Chase

Chases are not only about speed, the environment also plays a role. To keep the Chase dynamic, the Referee and players can incorporate obstacles using the following rules:

- Determine who is ahead based on the highest accumulated Effort.
- Characters ahead of others can introduce an obstacle or challenge, Make Test A Skill or Attribute and use the result as a threshold for others to overcome.
- Those behind must make the same skill check, to continue the pursuit.
- Failing the test simply removes Effort from your total equal to the Degree of Failure. But the obstacle is considered to be passed.
- A fumble removes a person from the chase, the Obstacle was too much for them.
- The Referee approves or adjust obstacles to maintain logic and pacing.

Example:

- A lead runner declares a jump over a fence,
- Those behind must make the same Test to stay in pursuit.
- A Strong character can declare to push over crates, A Fitness Attribute Test.
- Those behind would then have to make that Test.

**Examples of Obstacles:**

| Obstacle              | Example                 | Skill used                                               |
| --------------------- | ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Foot traffic          | Path, mall, alley       | Shadowing                                                |
| Minor obstacle        | Bench, slick floor      | Jumping, AGI                                             |
| Larger obstacle       | Boxes, stacks, shelves, | Jumping to avoid them, CON to ram through                |
| Foliage and Shrubbery | Park trail              | Camouflage for the lead and Observation for the pursuers |

## Mounted Combat

Some Chronicles allow mounted combat. Mounts provide mobility and can also act independently. Each mount has a Mount Skill which represents how trained it is to be ridden. Vehicles are meant to be mounted, they do not have a Mount Skill and use the riders Pilot Skill.

When a mount is stressed, the rider's Ride Skill effective Level and Complexity are limited by the mount's Mount Skill Level and Complexity. For casual travel and non stressful situations use the rider's Ride Skill.

### Mounting & Dismounting

Mounting uses 2 Take Time Actions, one getting into position, and one mounting. Dismounting uses 1 Take Time Action, if the mount is moving make a Jump Skill Test with a Threshold equal to.

| Speed     | Threshold |
| --------- | --------- |
| Adjusting | 1         |
| Crawling  | 2         |
| Walking   | 3         |
| Jogging   | 4         |
| Running   | 5         |
| Sprinting | 6         |

On Failure use the collision rules to determine damage using the Mount's size and Speed. The test uses the Degree of Failure as the defense roll against the collision. If there is a chance to slide of the surface of impact halve the base damage before rolling.

### Controlling the Mount

Mounts can be surprised, become scared and loose control.

- Make a CHA Riding Skill Test, with a Threshold of 7 - Riding Skill Lvl.
- The mount's Mount Skill does not affect this Test.
- Success: You keep control of the mount.
- Failure: The mount acts on instinct.
- Fumbles: You are forced to dismount, (falling).

A mount trained for combat can perform attacks using its \[Striking\]Unarmed skill.

### Mounted Combat Limitations

When attacking or defending:

- Use the lower of the rider's Enabled Attack Skill or Enabled Defense vs the Riding Skill.
- Collisions with a Thrusting weapons use the mount's Size and Speed, apply Collision Damage to the rider's weapon instead of the rider.
- The Rider can change facing to Attack but the Riding Skill takes penalties depending on his relation to the facing of the mount.
- Mounts usually only have Evasion as an Enabled Defense.

### Flying Mounted Combat

Flying mounted combat uses the same rules but the mount's effective Mount Skill is limited by their Flying Skill.

## Random Hit Locations

When a target has no clear definition but it is still possible to be attacked (e.g., a shadow, silhouette, vision impairment, or randomness) roll 2d6 and consult the table.

| Result | Location                   |
| ------ | -------------------------- |
| 2      | Eyes, Neck, Forehead (1d3) |
| 3      | Left-Feet                  |
| 4      | Left-hand                  |
| 5      | Left-Leg                   |
| 6      | Left-Arm                   |
| 7      | Torso                      |
| 8      | Right-Arm                  |
| 9      | Right-Leg                  |
| 10     | Right-hand                 |
| 11     | Right-Feet                 |
| 12     | Lung, Guts, Heart (1d3)    |

For the 1d3 rolls simply roll a 1d6 and apply the following to the result

| 1d6 | Head     | Vitals |
| --- | -------- | ------ |
| 1-2 | Eyes     | Lungs  |
| 3-4 | Neck     | Guts   |
| 5-6 | Forehead | Heart  |

### Avoiding Hit Locations

To avoid damaging a specific location, a character can use the Aim Action. The Attacker sacrifices one die gained from aiming to exclude one location from the hit roll (for example, avoiding the Head compartment). If the location roll results in the avoided area, reroll.

To intentionally target a system, use the standard Targeting rules.

## Attacking at Different Elevations

Elevation changes posture based penalties:

- If the Defender is lower than the Attacker's eye level (around waist level), treat the Defender as a Kneeling opponent.
- If the character is lower than the Attacker's waist level ( around their knees), treat the Defender as a Crawling opponent.
- Characters may not reach each other on the torso, depending on the weapon's reach, in that case they either must adjust their posture to reach, or use targeting rules.

## Researching, Books & Data

Books and data archives can serve as knowledge treasures. They grant bonus die.

- You need a number of books of the same topic equal to the square of the desired bonus.
- Your bonus is limited by the Skill Complexity Level.
- The Information must have a CPX Rating equal to the desired bonus.
- The Time taken researching equals one workday timeframe (2d6 hours) divided by the difference between Test CPX and Skill CPX.

## Tactical Scales

Different entities operate on different scales for damage and range. Use these when comparing humans, vehicles and colossal machines.

Scale Multiplier applies to:

- Damage Rating (xDR)
- Base Distance (BD)

For example, a Tank's canon can reach 30 hexes and do x10 damage, in the same way a Planetary cannon can reach 30 hexes and do x10 damage. But in their respective scales they are different. A single projectile of a planetary cannon could cover several solar systems in distance and its damage able to destroy the planets that could fit thousands of tanks in them.

Tactical Scales

| Size Range | Multiplier | Time | Movement | Comparison                 |
| ---------- | ---------- | ---- | -------- | -------------------------- |
| 0-5        | x1         | x1   | 1mt      | Human                      |
| 6-10       | x10        | x6   | 6mt      | Vehicle                    |
| 11-15      | x100       | x60  | 60mt     | Fantastical, Superweapons. |

### Larger Scales Acting on Smaller Scales

When a large scale unit attacks a smaller one.

- Add an Area Effect (1) SpecialFx to the attack. If it already has one add 1 to the Rating.
- Apply size penalties or use Targeting rules accordingly.

### Dueling Clash

Cinematic games can have combat turn into a brawl for a brief moment before returning to safer conventional combat. This is called a Dueling Clash. Machines can choose to enter each other's Hex and duel while combat happens. Lower the Scale by one step and treat the combat as if it was a miniature battle, this grants the people involved extra turns and actions according to the new timescale. Adjust their movement to the equivalent and play the fight out.

Referees should consider how extra turns impact the flow in the table. it is not a good idea to have a few players play while others simply watch.

## Timeframe

Some actions have a variable time to be completed, when the Referee wants to determine how long they would take they simple roll a timeframe. Remember, characters do not live their lives second by second as per the rules of the game.

### Taking more Time or Speeding Up

Characters can choose to take more or less time when performing an action. You can move the timeframe required by 1 step above (speed up) or below (take longer) and adjust accordingly.

- Speed up: loose 2D.
- Slow down: gain 2D.

## Searching Containers in a Hurry

Items in containers that are purposely organized like bandoleers, ammo pouches, or arrow quivers only take 1 second to take the desired object out of the container.

Characters in a hurry to look into their bags and containers, can use the following procedure:

| Container  | Required Time |
| ---------- | ------------- |
| Pouch      | 3 seconds     |
| Small bag  | 6 seconds     |
| Large Back | 12 seconds    |
| Backpack   | 18 seconds    |

- Decide how many seconds they shave off (minimum 1).
- Make a Sense Attribute Test with a threshold equal to the seconds shaved.
- You can use the Knowledge Attribute (if they own the container).

Results:

- Success: you shave the time off.
- Failure: You spill the container's contents.
