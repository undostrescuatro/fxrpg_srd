## When to Ask for a Test

Only ask for a test when the outcome truly matters, such as:

- When success or failure is uncertain.
- When performance over time affects the result of a task.

Do not roll in the following cases:

- For trivial actions without dramatic consequences.
- When success or failure can be reasonably assumed.

Examples:

- Crossing the street requires no roll, but chasing a target through heavy traffic does.
- Fixing your car at home does not need a roll, repairing it for a mission does.

As a general rule, only ask for a test when it adds or relieve tension in the game.

### Acting and Reacting Characters

In a Test, a Character's relationship to the action performed can affect the test:

- Acting Character: Initiates the action. For combat, this is the Attacker.
- Reacting Character: Is affected by the action. For combat, this is the Defender.

Certain tests favor the Acting Character while others favor the Reacting Character.

## Core Test Rules

Most test in FxRPG follow the same procedure. Perform these steps whenever the Referee calls for a test.

Before rolling:

- Identify the Acting Character.
- Determine the Threshold of the test.
  - Or if a Reacting Character is making an Opposed roll.
- Declare the Attribute or Skill being tested.

Performing the test:

- Roll a number of dice as determined by the Attribute or Skill tested.
- If the number of dice is 0, roll two dice and discard the highest dice.
- Otherwise, take the highest roll as your base number.
- For every remaining dice resulting in '6', add +1 to the total.
- Add any Attribute or Situational modifiers.

After the test:

- Compare the total to the Threshold, or the opposed test's total.
- If the Acting Character rolls higher, the difference is the Degree of Success (DoS).
- If the Acting Character rolls lower, the difference is the Degree of Failure (DoF).
- The Degree determines the quality and impact of the outcome.

Considerations:

- If all dice roll on 1, a Fumble happened.
- The number of dice can never be reduced below 0.
- A total can never be reduced below 0 by modifiers.

## Degree of Effect

The Degree is the most important part of the test, as it defines the context of the outcome.

If the roll was successful, then it is known as Degree of Success or "DoS", if the roll was a failure, then it is known as Degree of failure or "DoF".

The following table helps contextualize the quality of the action based on the Degree of Effect obtained.

| Degree | Description        | Narrative | Description                  |
| ------ | ------------------ | --------- | ---------------------------- |
| -3     | Impressive Failure | No, And.  | Memorable blunder.           |
| -2     | Great Failure      | No        | Embarrassing performance.    |
| -1     | Normal Failure     | No, But.  | Average mistake.             |
| 0      | Mixed outcome      | Depends   | Depends on the type of test. |
| +1     | Normal Success     | Yes, But. | Succeeded as expected.       |
| +2     | Great Success      | Yes       | Praiseworthy performance.    |
| +3     | Impressive Success | Yes, And. | Memorable achievement.       |

{: .note }
Narrative results are merely a guideline for opening chances of improvised consequences during gameplay. Results with a "But" add a consequence opposite to their result (success with consequences, failure with a respite). Results with an "And" add a consequence of the same type as the result (double Success/Failure).

## Using Your Edge Dice

Before each encounter, give each player at least 1 Edge Dice, or the gap between the number of players and the number of threats (traps, enemies, or challenges). Particularly tough threats may grant extra Edge Dice. This ensures every player has a fair chance, even against difficult odds.

Edge Dice can be spent at any time to boost a roll.

- Add the spent dice to your current roll (maximum 5 Edge Dice per roll).
- Unused Edge Dice may be converted into Experience Points at the end of the session.

To avoid Experience unbalance in characters here are optional rules:

- Each spent Edge Dice grants 1 free Experience Point.
- Create a fixed Edge Dice pool, and award Experience separately.

## Fumbles

Fumbles should represent bad luck or circumstances, not incompetence. They're meant to add tension or humor, not to humiliate the character. Even a Fumble can still lead to a form of success, just with an unexpected twist.

| Attribute    | Example Fumble Effects                                                |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Agility      | The ground shifts, the character looses balance or gets a cramp       |
| Constitution | Their own size hinders movement or balance                            |
| Fitness      | They loose breath, strain a muscle, or Feel sudden pain               |
| Appearance   | They sneeze, wrinkle clothes, or something ruin their outfit          |
| Sense        | They believe they sensed somethings. Like feeling watched, or touched |
| Creativity   | They make a wrong assumption or miscalculate                          |
| Knowledge    | They recall a fact incorrectly                                        |
| Focus        | They give momentarily to fear, pain, or temptation                    |
| Charisma     | They commit a social blunder (a faux Pas)                             |
| Instinct     | They panic, freeze, or misread a social clue                          |

## Considerations of a Test

When Testing consider two factors:

First, what is being tested:

- A Character's Attribute.
- A Character's Skill.

Second, how long the Test Takes:

- Action test: Quick actions, 1 second or less.
- Effort test: Short tasks, 1 minute or more.
- Work test: Extended tasks, 1 hour or more.

### Testing Attributes

Testing an Attribute measures a character's natural instinctive reaction. Meaning things that happen too quickly for conscious control or Skill use. These tests represent reflexes, Physical instincts, or immediate responses to sudden events.

To perform an Attribute Test:

- Use the Core Test Rules.
- The number of Dice equals to 2D.
- Add the relevant Attribute Rating.

{: .note }
Attribute Tests are similar to saving throws in other RPG systems.

#### Testing Primary Attributes

Here are some examples in which Attributes are tested:

- Agility Tests: Precision and quick movement. For example: Catching a ledge as the ground gives way.
- Constitution Tests: Body mass and structure. For example: Holding a door shut, or withstanding sudden impacts.
- Fitness Tests: Endurance or physical strain. For example: Hanging from a ledge, Working without rest, or resisting fatigue or Physical curses.
- Appearance Tests: Natural charm and first impressions. For example: Standing out in a crowd, or sparking attraction at first sight. Used in reaction rolls when meeting strangers.
- Sense Tests: Notice things trough the five senses. For example: Hearing a faint noise, Spotting motion, or catching a strange scent, or taste.

- Creativity Tests: Quick thinking and spontaneous problem solving. For example: Solving a puzzle on the fly or connecting clues in real time.
- Knowledge Tests: Used for Recalling information and retaining it. For example: Remembering formulas, historical facts, or learned procedures.
- Focus Tests: Mental fortitude. Example: Resisting pain, fear, or mental influence (including magic).
- Charisma Tests: Presence and strength of personality. For example: Commanding attention, winning a crowd, or speaking over others.
- Instinct Tests: Intuition, and emotional perception. For example: Feeling danger, reading emotions, or perceiving spirits. Used in Impression Rolls after social interactions to gauge of an NPC feels towards a PC.

{: .note }
Try not to rely on Creativity rolls to give players solutions to problems. Part of the fun of the game is having the players solve the problems instead of their characters.

{: .note }
Use Sense Tests for passive awareness and things that can be missed. A glimpse, whiff, a brief sound, or a weird flavor. For active search and observation use the skill.

#### Testing Evasion

Evasion is only tested when a character is aware of an incoming attack and has a chance to dodge it. Evasion is mainly used during Action Mode, or attacks.

- Use the Core Test Rules.
- The number of Dice equals to the Evasion Rating.

### Testing Skills

To Test a Skill:

- Use the Core Test Rules.
- The number of Dice equals to the Lvl of the Skill.
- Unskilled characters are considered Lvl 0.

#### Skill Attribute Modifier

Every Skill Test gets modified by one relevant Attribute, Only one Attribute applies based on the situation. This adds flexibility to Skills. For example:

A weapon skill can:

- Uses Sense to notice a strange sound.
- Uses Knowledge to identify the cause.
- Uses Creativity to repair it.

In some cases an attribute may apply negatively:

- A large Constitution may make it harder to squeeze into tight spaces.
- A striking Appearance may make it harder to hide.

Skill entries suggest common attribute modifiers, but creative use is always encouraged.

#### Complexity During Tests

Skill Tests have a Complexity Rating. This affects the Test in the following ways:

- Equal Complexity, Test as normal.
- Lower Skill Complexity, get a Dice Penalty equal to the difference.
- Higher Skill Complexity, does nothing, the benefit applies to other areas like new uses of a skill, new tools, or ways to use them.

Considerations:

- Complexity can never go below 0. Things can’t be less complex than Common knowledge.
- Opposed tests Compare each character's Complexity only to the action they attempt, not to their opponent.

## Personal Action Test

A Personal Action Test compares a dice roll to a fixed Threshold. They usually represent unchanging challenges like natural situations or challenges that remain consistent in their difficulty.

- A higher Threshold means greater difficulty.
- A Degree of 0 is a Success for the Acting Character.

### Personal Thresholds

| Threshold | Adjective      | Description                                                    |
| --------- | -------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 0         | Special        | Not a test, used as part of special rules.                     |
| 1         | Effortless     | Automatic success, unless penalties apply.                     |
| 2         | Routine        | Barely requires attention.                                     |
| 3         | Easy           | Needs a minimum focus.                                         |
| 4         | Moderate       | Standard Challenge, require focus.                             |
| 5         | Challenging    | Needs focus and skill.                                         |
| 6         | Difficult      | Demands focusing and good skill.                               |
| 7         | Very Difficult | Must focus, have good skill, talent, or a favorable situation. |
| 8         | Only the Best  | Needs high skill, talent, and a favorable situation.           |
| 10        | Impossible     | Achievable only through exceptional means                      |

Considerations:

- Threshold 4 is the average threshold commonly seen during play.
- Threshold 7+ are nearly impossible for an average character ( all Attributes at 0 Rating) unless aided by bonuses or Edge dice.
- Consider the context when setting Thresholds. How difficult is the task for an average human? If your players are special let them be special do not make a challenge harder just because your characters are stronger, let them be special and save the higher thresholds for epic circumstances.

## Opposed Action Test

Acting and Reacting Characters make their appropriate Test Roll, then Compare the results:

- The higher total wins.
- Determine the Degree of Effect as normal.
- A Degree of 0 is a Success for the Reacting Character.

## Group Test and Helping Others

Some tasks allow teamwork, where one person leads and others assist. Use the following steps:

- Choose a leader. This character makes the test.
- Assign Assistants. Each one adds one Dice '+1D' to the roll.
- If half or more of the assistants roll 1, Treat it as a Fumble regardless of the result.

Considerations:

- Physical tasks are limited by commonsense. For Example: Only so many people can push a car, but a lot can pull a single rope.
- Other skills are limited by a number of people equal to '2' to the power of the leader's CPX Rating. $(2^{CPX})$ or $(1,2,8,16,32)$ respectively. The assistants must have levels in the relevant skill.

## Effort Test

Effort tests measure how quickly a task can be completed, rather than how well it's done in an instant. Effort is used to track progress. Before starting an Effort Test, the Referee must establish a few key details:

- The Personal Threshold is always 0.
- Apply the test's Complexity as normal.
- Declare the required Effort.
- List one or more skills that can be used.

Running the test:

- Characters roll using the available skills.
- Add up their Degree of Effect to the Effort.
- If the total Effort meets or exceeds the required Effort, the task is finished successfully.
- If not, time passes and more Effort is required.

Considerations:

- Effort Tests usually take several rolls to finish.
- Low rolls means slow progress.
- High rolls mean fast progress.
- A fumble can happen on any roll.

## Work Test

A Work Test measures consistency and precision over time rather than instant performance. It's used for long term tasks that take hours or days to complete like repairing, crafting, or building.

Work Tests use Daily Work Points to track progress. Each Work Point represents one unit of productive work, Usually performed during the span of a regular 8 hour workday.

### Manual Labor

For physical work (manual labor), use this formula:

- Daily WP = 8+'Physical Attribute Rating'+'CON Rating'

For Mental work (paperwork), use this formula:

- Daily WP = 8+'Mental Attribute Rating'+'FOC Rating'

### Skilled Labor

For tasks that require skill, use this formula:

- Daily Skilled WP = (Skill Lvl)\*(8+'Skill CPX'-'Task CPX')

### Setting up a Work Test

Before Starting, first define:

- The Threshold. Representing the difficulty.
- The Complexity. The technical knowledge needed to solve the problem.
- The Work Points required. Total needed to finish the task.
- Relevant Skills. One or more that apply to the task.

Once the total Work Points required are known, calculate how many workdays it will take:

- Workdays needed = 'Total Work points needed'/8

After the required work period:

- The character makes a Personal Action Test roll as usual.
- The Degree determines the outcome as usual.

### Work Tests Fails

If the task fails. The Referee may reduce the total Work Points needed before trying again. For example: If the Threshold was 6 and the roll result was 3, reduce the remaining work points for the next try by half.
